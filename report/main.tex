\begin{abstract}
This paper presents the complete design flow of a MDAC stage for a medium-resolution, high-speed pipeline 
ADC implemented in IHP 130~nm CMOS usign open source tools. 
The target converter operates at 1.5V, 100~MS/s with 10-bit resolution and 500~mV$_{pp}$ 
differential output swing, with robust operation across process, voltage, and temperature (PVT) corners.
Starting from system-level requirements, we derive 
quantitative MDAC constraints on thermal noise, closed-loop settling, static gain 
accuracy, slew-rate, unity-gain bandwidth (UGBW), and phase margin for a given capacitive load.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Pipeline Analog-to-Digital Converters (ADCs) are widely employed in modern high-speed, medium-to-high resolution applications due to their favorable trade-off between sampling rate, power efficiency, and achievable accuracy.

This type of ADCs chieve high throughput by distributing quantization across multiple stages, each composed of a sub-ADC, sub-DAC, residue amplifier, and sample-and-hold function. The MDAC is the central block in each stage because it both reconstructs the coarse quantization level and amplifies the residue for the next stage. For an ideal 1.5-bit/stage implementation, the residue transfer is
\begin{equation}
V_{res}=2V_{in}-D\cdot V_{ref},
\end{equation}
where $D\in\{-1,0,+1\}$ is the digital decision and the gain factor of 2 is realized by capacitor ratio and closed-loop OTA action.

\subsection{Derived amplifier requirements}
\subsubsection{Gain}
For a closed-loop residue gain of 2, finite OTA gain $A_0$ introduces fractional gain error roughly
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_g\approx\frac{1}{\beta A_0},
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is the feedback factor (typically near 0.5 in a gain-of-2 MDAC). Requiring residue gain error to be significantly below 0.5 LSB referred to full scale leads to
\begin{equation}
\varepsilon_g\lesssim\frac{1}{2^{N+1}} \quad\therefore\quad A_0\gtrsim\frac{2^{N+1}}{\beta}.
\end{equation}
For $N=10$ and $\beta=0.5$, this implies $A_0\gtrsim 4096$ ($\approx72$~dB). With digital calibration and redundancy, relaxed targets are possible; nevertheless 70--75~dB open-loop gain is a robust design objective.

\subsubsection{Unity-Gain Bandwidth and Phase Margin}
At 100~MS/s, the clock period is 10~ns. With two non-overlapping phases, available settling time in amplification phase is approximately
\begin{equation}
T_{\phi2,eff}\approx 4\text{--}5\;\text{ns}
\end{equation}
after subtracting switch dead-time and comparator latching overhead.

For first-order settling,
\begin{equation}
\left|\varepsilon_{dyn}\right|\approx e^{-t/\tau},\qquad \tau\approx\frac{1}{2\pi\beta\,\text{UGBW}}.
\end{equation}
Imposing $\varepsilon_{dyn}<1/2^{N+1}$ at $t=T_{\phi2,eff}$ yields
\begin{equation}
\text{UGBW}\gtrsim \frac{\ln(2^{N+1})}{2\pi\beta T_{\phi2,eff}}.
\end{equation}
For $N=10$, $\beta=0.5$, and $T_{\phi2,eff}=4.5$~ns, UGBW is around 540~MHz.

\subsubsection{Thermal Noise and Sampling Capcitor}
The thermal noise introduced by the sampling switch is shaped by the RC network formed by the switch resistance $R$ and the sampling capacitor $C_s$, which behaves as a first-order low-pass filter with transfer function:

\begin{equation}
A(j\omega) = \frac{1}{1 + j\omega R C_s}
\end{equation}

The total sampled noise is obtained by integrating the filtered noise power spectral density over frequency:

\begin{equation}
\overline{e_n^2} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{0}^{\infty} \overline{e_R^2} \cdot |A(j\omega)|^2 \, d\omega
\end{equation}

Substituting the thermal noise of the resistor:

\begin{equation}
\overline{e_R^2} = 4kTR
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\overline{e_n^2} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{4kTR}{1 + (\omega R C_s)^2} \, d\omega
\end{equation}

Solving the integral:

\begin{equation}
\overline{e_n^2} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{4kTR}{R C_s} \cdot \left[ \arctan(\omega R C_s) \right]_{0}^{\infty} = \frac{kT}{C_s}
\end{equation}

Thus, the sampled noise depends only on the sampling capacitance:

\begin{equation}
\sigma_n^2 = \frac{kT}{C_s}
\end{equation}

Imposing the resolution constraint:

\begin{equation}
\sigma_n \le \frac{LSB}{2}, \quad LSB = \frac{V_{FS}}{2^N}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
C_s \ge \frac{kT}{(LSB/2)^2}
\end{equation}

In practice:

\begin{equation}
C_s \approx \alpha \cdot \frac{kT}{(LSB/2)^2}, \quad \alpha \approx 3\text{--}10
\end{equation}

\section{Amplifier design}
\subsection{Topology Selection}
A folded cascode OTA with PMOS input pair is selected to maximize input common-mode range and reduce flicker noise. The PMOS input devices allow operation with input voltages close to ground, which is suitable for switched-capacitor MDAC environments. The folded structure provides high gain while maintaining moderate voltage headroom under low supply conditions.

The signal path consists of a PMOS differential pair with transconductance $g_{m1}$, whose currents are folded into NMOS cascode devices, producing a high output resistance node.

\subsection{Gain}
For a folded-cascode stage, low-frequency gain can be approximated as
\begin{equation}
A_0 \approx g_{m1}R_{out},
\end{equation}
where $R_{out}$ is the small-signal resistance at the output node. With cascoding,
\begin{equation}
  R_{out} \approx \left(g_{m7,8}r_{O7,8}r_{O9,10}\right) \parallel \left[g_{m3,4}r_{O3,4}(r_{O5,6}\parallel r_{O1,2})\right].
\end{equation}
Hence, gain is increased primarily by: (i) maximizing $g_{m1}$, and (ii) increasing $r_O$ through longer channel devices.

\subsection{Gain enchancement}
To meet gain under 130~nm intrinsic gain limits, gain enhancement (gain-boosting) is applied to NMOS cascode transistors. If auxiliary amplifiers provide local gain $A_1$, then effective output resistance scales approximately as
\begin{equation}
R_{out,eff} \approx R_{out}\,(1+A_1),
\end{equation}
Practical implementation must preserve stability; therefore gain-boosting amplifier poles should be placed sufficiently above MDAC closed-loop bandwidth, or compensated to avoid excess phase lag near unity crossover.


The auxiliary amplifier itself is implemented using a folded cascode topology, which provides high gain. Because the ouput swing is relative small, to stabilize its output common-mode voltage, a continuous-time common-mode feedback (CMFB) network based on MOS transistors operating in the triode region is employed. These devices act as voltage-controlled resistors, setting the output common-mode level without introducing significant additional poles.
\subsection{Frequency response}
The frequency response of the folded cascode OTA is analyzed using the node-pole association method, which allows identifying the dominant poles by associating each high-impedance node with its corresponding resistance and capacitance. Neglecting gain boosting effects, the response is dominated by three main nodes: the output node, the internal folding node $X$, and the input node.

The dominant pole is typically located at the output due to the large load capacitance $C_L$ and high output resistance $R_{out}$:

\begin{equation}
\omega_{p,out} \approx \frac{1}{R_{out} C_L}
\end{equation}

The internal node $X$, corresponding to the folded branch (drain of the input pair and source of the cascode devices), introduces a non-dominant pole given by:

\begin{equation}
\omega_{p,X} \approx \frac{1}{R_X C_X}
\end{equation}

with:

\begin{equation}
  R_X \approx \frac{1}{g_{m3,4}}, \quad C_X \approx C_{gs3,4} + C_{sb3,4} + 2C_{gd5,6} + C_{db5,6}
\end{equation}

Stability requires sufficient separation between $\omega_{p,out}$ and the higher-frequency poles at node $X$ and the input, indicating the need of a high $g_{m3,4}$ to ensure a phase margin greater than $60^\circ$.

At the input, the pole is defined by the source resistance and the total input capacitance:

\begin{equation}
\omega_{p,in} \approx \frac{1}{R_{in} C_{in}}
\end{equation}

where:

\begin{equation}
R_{in} \approx \frac{1}{g_{m1}}, \quad C_{in} \approx C_{gs1} + C_{gd1}(1 + A_v)
\end{equation}

The Miller multiplication of $C_{gd1}$ can significantly increase the effective input capacitance, although in switched-capacitor applications this effect is partially mitigated by the sampling network.

Overall, the unity-gain bandwidth is primarily determined by:

\begin{equation}
UGBW \approx \frac{g_{m1,2}}{C_L}
\end{equation}
To reach a UGBW of 540~MHz for a load of $C_L=1.25$ pF, the $g_{m1,2}$ will be
\begin{equation}
  g_{m1,2} &= 2\pi 540\,\text{MHz} \times 1.25\,\text{pF} = 4.2\,\text{mS} \\
\end{equation}
Considering a $g_m/I_{D}=12$
\begin{equation}
  I_{D_{M0}} = \frac{4.2\text{mS}}{12} \approx 350\,\text{$\mu$A}
\end{equation}
However, as it will be described in the next section, this current might be higher due to the slew-rate requirements.

\subsection{Switched capacitor common mode feedback}
The switched-capacitor common-mode feedback (SC-CMFB) circuit regulates the output common-mode voltage by periodically sensing and correcting it through charge redistribution. During phase $\phi_1$, the sampling capacitors acquire the output common-mode level:

\begin{equation}
V_{CM,out} = \frac{V_{O1} + V_{O2}}{2}
\end{equation}

In phase $\phi_2$, the capacitors are reconnected to the reference common-mode voltage $V_{CM,ideal}$, generating an error proportional to:

\begin{equation}
\Delta V_{CM} = V_{CM,out} - V_{CM,ideal}
\end{equation}

This error is transferred to the control node $V_{CMFB}$, which adjusts the OTA biasing (e.g., $M_{5,6}$ gate voltage), closing the feedback loop and forcing:

\begin{equation}
V_{CM,out} \rightarrow V_{CM,ideal}
\end{equation}

The switched-capacitor implementation ensures accurate common-mode sensing and is inherently compatible with discrete-time operation. Proper capacitor matching and non-overlapping clock phases are required to minimize offset, charge injection, and instability in the CMFB loop.
